Mitsubishi Heavy Industries - my failed success

December 26, 2024

The pandemic was a seismic event for the entire market. Stocks stayed cheap for quite some time - some even lingered at bargain-bin prices longer than others. Industrials, for example. And what fascinates me - at least personally - is how people manage to become absurdly pessimistic precisely when things hit rock bottom. Take Mitsubishi Heavy Industries (MHI) trading at 2500 or so in 2021. People were practically eulogizing the company, saying, “This company is dead, and here’s a 1000-tweet thread explaining why (1/1000).” There are so many such idiots. I’m not exaggerating, but if these kinds of idiots were told to eat shit, humanity would instantly lose its classification as omnivores.

Mitsubishi Heavy axed its regional jet program in 2021—an ambitious money pit that bled them dry year after year while showing no glimmer of hope. Naturally, the dung-eaters erupted in celebration, shouting, “This company has no future! It’s dying!” Oh, absolutely. I can still vividly recall December 2022, when CRM slashed its workforce in a brutal culling. Michael Burry, never one to miss a chance at being wrong in public, rushed to Twitter to proclaim that the stock should be down instead of up after such layoffs: “CRM should have been down 25% on those job cuts. Job cuts are so not the reason to own that.” Oh, this one is perhaps bigger than his big short - CRM has gone up from $130 to $344! That's higher return than his short on subprime! - And achieved in a shorter period.

And just as vividly, I remember a few months ago when Boeing cut its workforce—and its stock shot straight up. But wait, let’s not forget James Chanos, the self-proclaimed “Enron killer,” chiming in with his pearls of wisdom. “In the dot-com bubble, markets also reacted positively to job cuts, and look what happened.” Sure, Mr. Chanos is so brilliant that he had to shut down his funds because of - what’s the term? - shitty returns.

Ah, and how could I forget John Hempton? A short seller from Australia who barks at everything！ He was pounding the table with bearish takes when Rolls-Royce’s stock bottomed at 70 and swapped its CEO. “It’s probably not gonna work because the last five CEOs all failed.” Brilliant insight. Meanwhile, the stock now trades at 575..

You don’t bet against a company making a redemption - why? Because companies, unlike the armchair doomsayers, actually have a will to survive. In short, if the company fails, the stock has already priced that in. And if the company does go through difficulties, the stock price goes up bigtime. But go ahead - keep betting against them. It’s your nature, after all. And just like Buffett said, you don’t try to be a short seller. It poisons your mindset and leaves you retarded. Now, I don't think you'll be surprised at all that these dummies are calling it the AI bubble. "No one rings a bell at the top, but I have good feelings that the bubble topped yesterday." You know what? I love clowns.

The thesis for MHI was extremely simple. It is the biggest defense contractor in Japan. For both cultural and practical reasons it cannot fail. After cutting its regional jet program, it was trading at ~6X forward earnings for quite some time. I bought MHI at 2100 (the exact bottom!!!) and sold it at 4600 because I was afraid of a recession. See, I was both a macro and doomsayer. And I ate X2 amount of shit for my mistakes.

The moral of my story, I suppose, is that investing is all about being optimistic at the right times. It’s never about always being right - and I’ll try to explain why.

When you focus on trying to be right or appear smart, you end up trying to predict the future. The only way to do that is by quoting history and making comparisons. However, history never repeats itself exactly, and plenty of details can be overlooked. History often fosters bearishness, yet markets are full of pump-and-dump cycles.

You want to be right and avoid looking foolish. So, you hold back your bearishness when prices are rising and get loud about it when prices are falling. Then, when the next pump comes, you end up going broke.
